# Writing Style — Research & Documentation

All research documents, feature specs, knowledge base articles, and strategy docs follow **TechCrunch format**.

## The Rules

1. **Lead with the punchline.** First paragraph tells you everything. Rest is evidence.
2. **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences max. White space is your friend.
3. **Strong opinions, loosely held.** State the thesis. Back it with data or reasoning. Acknowledge counterpoints.
4. **No filler.** Cut "In order to", "It is important to note that", "As we can see". Just say the thing.
5. **Active voice.** "Intermind generates content" not "Content is generated by Intermind".
6. **Concrete over abstract.** Numbers, examples, names. Not "many coding agents" — "Claude Code, Codex, and Cursor".
7. **Section headers are sentences**, not labels. "Stdio servers don't auto-reconnect, so Intermind must crash safely" not "Stdio Reliability Considerations".
8. **Bold the key phrase** in important paragraphs so skimmers get 80% of the value.
9. **No walls of attributes/fields.** If listing attributes, keep it brief — 3-5 bullet points max inline. Full schemas go in appendices or implementation docs.
10. **Diagrams > tables > bullets > prose** for complex relationships.
11. **End sections with "So what?"** — what does this mean for the reader/builder/user.
12. **Sources inline.** Link or cite where claims come from. No unsourced statistics.

## Structure Template

```markdown
# Title — Subtitle That Tells You The Thesis

_One-line hook._

## The Big Idea (2-3 paragraphs)

## Section 1: Strong Claim as Header
Evidence. Example. So what.

## Section 2: Strong Claim as Header
Evidence. Example. So what.

## Where We Stand (or: What This Means for Intermind)

## Open Questions

---
_Last updated: {date}_
```

## Anti-Patterns

- Attribute dumps disguised as architecture docs
- "Comprehensive" lists that nobody reads
- Passive academic tone
- Headers that are just nouns ("Demographics", "Features", "Architecture")
- Sections longer than a phone screen scroll
